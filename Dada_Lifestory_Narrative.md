# **A Life Well Lived: The Story of Kailash Nath Bhargav**

*A narrative account compiled from oral history, recorded February 2026*

---

## **Prologue: The Man Who Never Had to Run**

There is a particular kind of man who does not chase the world. He does not send applications into the void or lobby for positions or scramble after opportunity. Instead, he lives well — with discipline, with generosity, with a quiet and unshakeable faith — and somehow, mysteriously, the world arranges itself around him. Doors open that he never knocked on. Jobs appear the morning after exams. Bank positions are offered over morning walks. Throughout his long life, Kailash Nath Bhargav would say, simply: *"Maine koi chala ke apply nahi kiya. Automatic matlab saari cheezein aayi hai."* I never ran around applying. Everything came on its own.

This is the story of how that happened — and why.

## **Chapter One: Diggi Mohalla**

### *Beawar, Rajasthan, 1941*

On the thirteenth of February, 1941, in the ancient walled city of Beawar, a boy was born into the Bhargav family. Beawar sat in the heart of Rajasthan, 180 kilometers from Jaipur, a city of merchants, masons, and old families who had lived in the same lanes for generations. The boy's family lived in a neighborhood called Diggi Mohalla, on a lane so thoroughly populated by Bhargavs that the locals simply called it *Bhargav Gali* — Bhargav Street.

The house was a traditional haveli, the kind that city families had built for centuries. Entering through the main lane, visitors would pass the ground floor, where a tenant named Devilal lived — a courtworker who paid rent and kept quietly to himself. The family occupied the upper floor, where rooms were arranged in an L-shape around a central space. There was the front room, then a side room, and then another side room along the angle of the L. At the deepest corner sat a small *kothri* — a compact inner chamber — and it was in this room, tucked away from the street's noise, that Kailash Nath Bhargav first drew breath.

Above the main floor, a rooftop terrace opened to the sky, and it would become the child's first kingdom.

His father, Mahesh Prasad Bhargav, was a man of education and standing. Across the lane lived a neighbor who happened to share nearly the same name — Mahesh Datt Bhargav, a lawyer of some reputation — and so the two families, linked by surname and separated only by the width of a gali, went about their lives in close proximity. Nearby lived Kaushal Kishore Bhargav and Ram Kishore Bhargav, the latter being the father of a boy who would grow up to be a lifelong friend. The neighborhood was not merely a place of residence; it was a living network of kinship and obligation, of shared celebrations and shared sorrows.

The boy who would be called Kailash grew up surrounded by all of this. His younger brother Shambhu arrived about two and a half years later, and together they formed the core of a childhood that would, in time, expand into a sprawling family of nine siblings.

### *The Earliest Memory*

Of all the things that a child might carry forward from those first years of life — the smell of the kitchen, a mother's voice, the quality of evening light — Kailash Nath Bhargav's earliest memory is a small, precise, and somehow perfect thing.

He was four years old, and his brother Shambhu was not yet three.

In one of the upper-floor rooms there was a *chabutra* — a raised platform, about two and a half feet high — positioned beneath a window that overlooked the chowk outside. From that window, one could watch the whole life of the neighborhood: vendors and neighbors, children and elders, the slow commerce of ordinary days. The chabutra was just high enough to require a small effort to climb.

Shambhu could do it. At barely three years old, the younger boy would haul himself up without any great difficulty, settle cross-legged, and peer out the window with the satisfied expression of one who has arrived somewhere important. Kailash, a full year older, could not. He would try — stepping up, stretching, scrambling — and the platform would defeat him each time.

It is a modest memory. There is no drama in it, no danger, nothing that would register as significant in the great catalog of human experience. And yet it has stayed with him for more than eight decades, clear as the day it happened. Perhaps what makes it worth keeping is precisely its smallness: a boy and his younger brother, a window and a platform, the ordinary competition of childhood, and an autumn afternoon in Beawar when the world was still very new.

## **Chapter Two: The Weight of a Name**

### *The Bhargav Family and Its Legacy*

To understand Kailash Nath Bhargav, one must first understand the world that made him — and in particular, the towering figure of his great-grandfather, a man whose legacy would shade every generation that followed.

His name was Dewan Bahadur Pyarilal Ji Bhargav, and he was, by any measure, an extraordinary man.

In the days of British India, when the region of Ajmer-Merwara functioned as a Central Government district administered directly from Delhi, Pyarilal Ji rose to become the Excise and Income Tax Commissioner — the man responsible, in practical terms, for generating and collecting the revenue that kept the administration functioning. He was, in modern language, a chief financial officer of a region, and the British recognized his abilities with the title *Dewan Bahadur*, an honorific reserved for those who had rendered exceptional service to the Crown.

But it is not for his government work that history remembers him in Beawar. It is for what he built.

Dewan Bahadur Pyarilal Ji gave the city a school. The Sanatan Dharm School, founded with his personal funds and his personal influence, became the institution where generations of Beawar's children would receive their education — including, in time, his great-grandson Kailash. He gave the city a college. The Sanatan Dharm College, now known as SD Government College, rose on land he donated. He had the entire building constructed at his own expense. And then — in a gesture that still strikes the family with something approaching awe when they think about it — he donated one hundred bighas of land to the institution. One hundred bighas. At today's prices, each bigha in that area carries a value of one and a half to two lakh rupees. The gift was staggering.

For himself, he kept twenty-eight bighas. A garden. A *bageecha*. A place of green and shade on the edge of a Rajasthani city where summer heat could be merciless.

This was the inheritance Kailash Nath Bhargav was born into — not of money, exactly, for the money had largely been given away, but of reputation, of expectation, of a family name that carried genuine weight in the town. When Mor Singh, a dacoit operating in the hills near Kharwa, said that he had eaten the salt of Dewan Bahadur Sahab and would therefore protect a member of the family, he was speaking a truth about what that name meant: that generosity on a large scale creates loyalties that outlive the man who gave the gifts.

### *The Family Tree*

Kailash's paternal lineage ran deep. His grandfather was Sitaram Ji Bhargav, who had come to Beawar from Jodhpur after an early and unexpected death left the family to find its footing anew. His great-grandfather was Kanhaiya Lal Ji Bhargav, a man with five sons: Radha Raman Ji, who became associated with government service in Beawar; Kalyan Kanwar Ji, whose line continued through Gopal Bhai Sahab's family; Sitaram Ji himself; Mohan Lal Ji, who eventually shifted to Gwalior; and Mukut Bihari Lal Ji, whose three sons — Shail Bihari, Kunj Bihari, and Banke Bihari — scattered to different cities, one settling in Bhopal.

Going further back: Kanhaiya Lal Ji's father was Harbaksh Singh Bhargav, a name that represents the furthest reach of the family's documented memory.

Of his grandfather Sitaram Ji, there is a remarkable story — one that speaks to the family's recurring willingness to sacrifice personal advantage for the sake of others. Sitaram Ji had been working on the railways in Jodhpur, where a proposal arose that he should marry the daughter of a prominent family. The girl, named Rema Bhargav, was the only child of her father Laxmi Narayan Ji. The prospective match-maker, from the girl's side, raised a concern: Rema had no sister. His family would not agree to the union without someone to stand in the role of a brother for the girl at her wedding.

Sitaram Ji's response was extraordinary. He agreed to be formally adopted into Laxmi Narayan Ji's family — to become, legally and socially, Rema's brother — so that the marriage could proceed. He gave up his place in a family of five brothers and became, through this sacrifice, the sole heir of three different households. For Laxmi Narayan Ji had a brother named Hira Lal Ji, and Hira Lal Ji had two sons: Chimman Lal Ji and Pyarilal Ji. And Pyarilal Ji — the Dewan Bahadur himself — had no children of his own to inherit what he had built.

The mathematics of sacrifice: by stepping away from the house of five brothers, Sitaram Ji stepped into the inheritance of three. The college, the school, the gardens — all of this eventually flowed through him to the generations that followed.

Sitaram Ji died young, at forty-five, when his son Mahesh Prasad — Kailash's father — was only twelve years old. The family relocated from Jodhpur to Beawar, and Mahesh Prasad Bhargav grew up in the shadow of his grandfather's legacy, understanding perhaps better than most what it means to be the steward of a name.

## **Chapter Three: The Summer of 1947**

### *A Six-Year-Old's Education in History*

The summer of 1947 was not like other summers. Even a child of six could feel it — the air had changed, thickened with something that the adults couldn't quite name out loud. The grownups spoke in lowered voices and moved with a careful urgency, and there were nights when Kailash lay on the rooftop under the stars and heard sounds drifting up from the street that he didn't fully understand.

August 15th came, and India was free.

For Kailash Nath Bhargav, the date arrived wrapped in both joy and terror, as it did for so many families in those weeks. The transfer of populations between the new nations of India and Pakistan was unfolding with a violence that no one had fully anticipated. Trains rolled in both directions across the border, and sometimes the trains that arrived from Pakistan were carrying not living passengers but the dead — Hindus who had been killed on their journey, their bodies sent back in the same wagons that had once carried them west with hope.

In Beawar, the family waited for news of Om Bhai Sahab — the son of Kailash's eldest aunt, a young man who had been traveling from Ajmer when the chaos overtook him. Curfew had been imposed. The streets were dangerous. There was only one way to get news: the lawyer across the lane, Mahesh Datt Bhargav, had a telephone. Kailash's father would lean through the small window cut into the wall between their houses and call out to the lawyer, asking him to place another call, to find out whether Om Bhai Sahab's train had departed, whether there was any word.

The calls went unanswered for hours. Then came news of something remarkable.

Near a village called Kharwa, in the hills outside Beawar, there operated a dacoit named Mor Singh. He lived under the protection of the local Kharwa Thakur and was by any conventional measure a man outside the law. But when Mor Singh saw train after train arriving from Pakistan carrying the bodies of dead Hindus, something hardened in him. He sent two of his men onto the train from Ajmer-to-Pakistan route, positioned near the driver. At Kharwa, the train was stopped. Every passenger was brought onto the platform. Hindus on one side, Muslims on the other — the sorting done with rough precision, identities questioned and confirmed.

Om Bhai Sahab stood on the platform without his *janeu* — the sacred thread that would have marked him unmistakably as Hindu. He had no obvious marker of his faith. When Mor Singh's voice rang out — *"Are you Hindu?"* — he answered without hesitation.

*"Yes, I am Hindu."*

*"Where are you going?"*

*"To the bageecha of Dewan Bahadur Pyarilal Ji. My uncle, Mahesh Prasad Bhargav, lives there."*

There was a pause. And then Mor Singh spoke words that the family would repeat for generations: *"Go. I have eaten the salt of Dewan Bahadur Sahab. You may pass freely. I will send one of my men to escort you all the way to Beawar."*

And so it was. A dacoit's code of loyalty — the old compact of salt, of *namak*, of the debt owed to those whose bread you had eaten — saved a man's life on the day India was born. The escort traveled with Om Bhai Sahab all the way to the city gates, and when he finally arrived at the family home, the relief that broke over the household was physical, like a pressure releasing from the chest.

Kailash Nath Bhargav was six years old when he witnessed this. He would never forget it.

### *Violence at Chand Gate*

The partition was not only a distant tragedy. It arrived in Beawar itself, in the streets and *mohallas* Kailash knew from childhood.

Near Chand Gate, on the road that led toward the family garden, a Muslim man was traveling through the city in a tonga one afternoon. The city had already seen violence, had already heard about what was happening on the trains, had already absorbed the shock of what humans could do to one another when faith became weaponized. A group of Arya Samajis — members of the Hindu reform movement who had their own properties near Chand Gate — stopped the tonga, pulled the man out, and killed him. Their reasoning, such as it was: *"You are killing Hindus. So we will kill you."*

It was retribution. It was also murder. And it was ordinary, by the standards of that extraordinary summer.

Kailash Nath Bhargav carries these memories as part of who he is — not as wounds, exactly, but as lessons. He saw what happened when communities turned on each other. He saw what happened when one man's loyalty to a generous ancestor saved another man's life. The contrast was instructive. Generosity generates protection. Hatred generates more hatred.

## **Chapter Four: The Garden and the Steel Body**

### *Life at the Bageecha, 1951 Onwards*

Around 1951, when Kailash was approximately ten years old, the family moved from the city house in Diggi Mohalla to the family garden — the *bageecha* that Dewan Bahadur Pyarilal Ji had kept for himself when he donated the rest of his landholdings to the college. Twenty-eight bighas of green, with a mali, with servants, with trees and space and the particular luxury of room to move.

After the crowded lanes of the walled city, the garden felt enormous.

But Kailash Nath Bhargav was not a boy who lounged in luxury. If anything, the move to the garden ignited in him something practical, something agricultural, something that wanted to be useful with his hands. He was ten years old, approximately, when he decided to start raising animals.

First: two cows. Then two buffaloes. Then, with a kind of escalating ambition that would characterize his approach to most things, thirteen goats.

Every morning, before the sun had properly risen, Kailash would be up. Four o'clock, sometimes five — while the rest of the household slept and the garden sat in its cool predawn quiet. He would go to the animals, spread the fodder, fill the water troughs, and begin milking. Two cows, two buffaloes — the milking alone could take the better part of an hour, and Shambhu would help, though Kailash always did more. Then the goats: thirteen of them, patient and noisy and each requiring individual attention.

The milk that came from this operation was prodigious. About eight kilos a day, churned into butter. And the butter was eaten — eaten the way only a growing boy in a garden, with unlimited access to fresh dairy and a body being built for physical life, can eat. Kailash would consume a *katora* — a bowl — of fresh butter in a sitting. Approximately two hundred grams. Then three kilos of milk, sometimes poured into a *bhagona* and drunk directly. Then another *katora* of fresh ghee, mixed with a little sugar. Then more milk on top.

He was eighteen years old when he made a bet — the details of which he remembers with a certain satisfaction even now — and won it by drinking five liters of milk in a single sitting.

The body that was built by this regimen was, as he puts it, extraordinary. Broad, strong, radiantly healthy, with the kind of complexion that made people stop and look twice. When he was taken as a child of six or seven to visit relatives in Ajmer, the neighbors in the Bhargav colony there would come to see him the way people come to see something exceptional. They would call out: *"Kahan hai Babbu?"* — using his pet name — or sometimes *Matru*, the nickname the Ajmer Bhargavs had given him, pressing their hands to his cheeks and pulling his face toward them in the way that adults cannot help doing with a child of exceptional beauty.

He did not particularly enjoy this. He was small, and they were large, and there was not much he could do about it.

### *The Red Tomatoes*

One of the purest pleasures of the garden childhood was the winter harvest of tomatoes.

In the cold months, the fields near the bageecha would produce tomatoes — deep red, fully ripe, heavy with juice. Kailash and Shambhu would rise early, before breakfast, before chores, and run directly from their rooms into the field. They wore only undershirts and underclothes in the cold morning air. They didn't wash the tomatoes. They picked them directly from the vine — warm from the night, yielding under the thumb — and ate them standing in the field, one after another, until they had consumed a kilo, sometimes two, sometimes more.

There were moolis and gajar too — radishes and carrots, pulled from the earth and carried back to be washed and eaten, their cold dirt-scented freshness filling the nose in the early morning. These were the breakfasts that built the body Kailash would carry through eight decades of living.

## **Chapter Five: School and the Games of Bhargav Gali**

### *Sanatan Dharm School*

The school his great-grandfather had built was the school Kailash attended, beginning in the second standard — he had been taught at home through first class and came to formal schooling already knowing the basics. The teachers were, in the close-knit world of Beawar Bhargavs, often relatives. His first teacher, Ram Prasad Ji Bhargav, walked with a slight limp and was a relation through his grandfather's line.

Every morning, Kailash would run to school. Not walk — run, or as close to running as sustained travel allowed. The route took him through familiar streets, past familiar houses. The school itself sat in Shahpura Mohalla, facing the house of the Rathi family — and the Rathis, Kailash would learn over time, had become the de facto custodians of the institution after his grandfather's early death had left Mahesh Prasad too young to take a seat on the board of trustees. It was a small injustice of history, of the kind that institutional memory can both preserve and blunt.

But the school was good. The teaching was serious. And half-time — the thirty-minute break in the middle of the school day — was an occasion for something close to organized joy.

### *Half-Time*

The group assembled like clockwork: Kailash, and his friends Anand Prakash Bhargav, Chandra Prakash Bhargav, Anil Kishore Bhargav — three brothers of different generations — along with a boy named Arjun who is alive still, and Gopal Chhapra, and sometimes others. Five, six, seven boys in the school courtyard, thirty minutes, no supervision worth mentioning.

They played *satoiiya* — the game of stacking stones and scattering them — and *langdi tang*, where a player hopped on one foot trying to touch the others. They ran after each other. They played *kho-kho*, the game of chains and escape. They shouted and sweated and crashed into each other and generally conducted themselves as boys have always conducted themselves when briefly freed from the requirement to sit still.

A snack vendor came to school sometimes, carrying samosas made fresh at home, warm and fragrant. Kailash's father had been clear on this: nothing bought on the street, nothing from the bazaar. Hands of unknown cleanliness, ingredients of uncertain provenance. The family brought their food from home, packed in a *katordaan* — a tiffin carrier of stacked containers — and that was what they ate.

He did not feel deprived. The roti from home was good. The company was better.

## **Chapter Six: The NCC Years**

### *A Sergeant at Fourteen*

When Kailash reached the eighth standard, approximately fourteen years old, he joined the National Cadet Corps — the NCC, the youth military service organization that trained India's schoolchildren in discipline, fitness, and the basics of soldiering.

He took to it immediately.

The NCC's junior division meant three years of training, and Kailash went through all of it with the characteristic intensity he brought to physical activity. They were drilled, marched, run, and exercised. They learned parade formations and military bearing. They were made to do things that tired the body until the body stopped complaining and simply performed.

By the time Kailash reached his later years in the junior division, he held the rank of sergeant. He was the one calling out the parade commands, leading formations of his classmates across the school grounds in the Rajasthan morning heat. A boy of fourteen or fifteen, voice just settling into its lower register, wearing his NCC uniform and directing people twice his size to *left turn* and *about face*.

He had photographs of this period. He kept them.

The NCC also gave him something else: tea.

At home, tea was a carefully rationed commodity. When it was available, the serving was perhaps half a small cup — a *chamach* or two, just enough to taste. But at NCC training, they served tea in full mugs, the big tin kind, deep brown and strong. On the days he trained, Kailash would drink his full mug and feel, for the first time, the particular alertness that caffeine provides. His tiredness would lift. The world would sharpen.

This discovery would become critically useful during examinations, when sleep deprivation and the demands of study made the body want only to collapse. He would skip dinner entirely on some exam nights, drinking tea and pushing through, sacrificing rest for retention.

### *The College NCC*

When Kailash moved to college, he joined the senior division of the NCC and remained for five years. He was promoted to Underofficer — the highest non-commissioned rank in the organization, equivalent to Subedar in the regular army.

His excellence was not lost on those in a position to act on it. When the 1962 war with China created an urgent need for NCC instructors in the military, a selection process was opened. Kailash Nath Bhargav was selected for a commission — not merely as a cadet, but as a commissioned officer, a step that would have taken him directly into the Indian Army.

He looked at the offer. He considered it. In the end, other paths opened before the commission could be finalized, and his life moved in a different direction. But the selection itself — the fact of having been found worthy of a commission — is part of the record of who he was at twenty-one.

## **Chapter Seven: The Art of the Game**

### *Basketball, Sweat, and the Bucket of Water*

By the time Kailash reached college, he had played cricket and hockey and kabaddi and kho-kho and run races and thrown javelins and competed in slow-cycle races and fast-cycle races, earning second and third prizes in the track events where his brother Shambhu usually edged him out for first. He was a versatile and natural sportsman — the kind of athlete who could be dropped into almost any game and figure out what was needed.

But his great love was basketball.

Basketball, as Kailash describes it, is a game that *takes* something from you. After thirty minutes on the court, sometimes an hour, the sweat would pour off him with a completeness that amazed people. His banyaan — his undershirt — could be twisted and wrung like a wet cloth. He claimed, and it seemed likely, that a full wringing could produce half a bucket of water.

This was the exertion he loved. Total commitment. Nothing held back.

The problem with total physical commitment is that it requires total physical recovery, and Kailash Nath Bhargav slept the way only the truly exhausted can sleep — with a depth and thoroughness that made waking him an engineering challenge. His father, when it came time to rouse him for morning study or duties, reportedly went through the following sequence: call, shake, louder call, louder shake, and then, when all conventional measures had failed, a full bucket of cold water poured directly onto the sleeping boy. Sometimes two buckets.

Sometimes the second bucket was still not enough.

The body had been worked until it demanded rest, and it was not taking no for an answer.

### *Athletes Carry Athletes Away*

College life had a recurring structure: Kailash would resolve to focus on studies, and then someone would discover, or remember, that he was an exceptional sportsman. Teams were assembling for tournaments — in Jodhpur, in Ajmer, in Bikaner, in Jaipur. They needed players. They needed *this* player. They would come and collect him with an enthusiasm he found difficult to refuse, and off he would go, representing his college, his city, his sports association, while the books waited patiently at home.

This was the rhythm of his college years: games punctuated by study, study interrupted by games. It did not make him an exceptional academic. But it made him something else — fit, capable, known, respected, and connected to a network of people across Rajasthan who had seen him play and trusted what they saw.

## **Chapter Eight: A Family of Nine**

### *The Crowded Room*

It is important, in a story about a man who later became a bank manager and lived in proper bank bungalows in Jodhpur and Udaipur and Kota, to understand where he came from.

Nine siblings. Their parents. Their grandmother. All of them, at various points, occupying the same space in Beawar — a space that was considerably reduced by the dispute with their uncle, who exercised his own claims to the family property with a selfishness that the wider family found difficult to understand or forgive. The uncle who had been sent to study labor law in England, who had risen to become a Labour Commissioner, who had benefited enormously from the sacrifices of his elder brother Mahesh Prasad — this same uncle who, when it came to the family house, said flatly that he wanted the better portion, and took it.

The result was that Kailash, his eight siblings, his parents, and his grandmother occupied two rooms in a three-room house, with the third room locked against them. Nine children sleeping in rows, like berths in a railway carriage, a system worked out of pure necessity.

Years later, Kailash would tell his grandchildren about this period not with bitterness but with a kind of clarity — a sense that having come from such circumstances, having built oneself from such a foundation, there was very little left to be afraid of.

### *The Cottage Industry*

Mahesh Prasad Bhargav was a single-income household supporting a very large household, and he understood that this arithmetic required supplementation. So the family started making things.

Candles, first. Not in small quantities — in hundred-kilo batches. The molds were metal, designed for the *Diwali* season when demand peaked, and the process was physical, repetitive, and satisfying: heating the wax, filling the molds, threading the wicks, releasing the finished candles, stacking them, and starting again. Kailash, Shambhu, their younger brother Naresh, and their sister Usha would work through these batches with focused speed. Two hundred candles in a day, sometimes more — small ones, the size of a finger, dozens to a tray.

Then bindis — the forehead decorations worn by Hindu women. Then nail polish. A small inventory of beauty products, manufactured in a garden house in Beawar, sold through channels Mahesh Prasad had developed, the profits flowing back to feed and clothe a family of eleven.

It was not glamorous work. It was honest work. And Kailash Nath Bhargav learned from it something that his formal economics education would later confirm: that production, however small-scale, is dignified. That making a thing is better than waiting for a thing to be given to you.

## **Chapter Nine: The Road to Jaipur**

### *Ajmer First*

For his B.Com Previous studies, Kailash came first to Ajmer, arriving with the modest resources of a young man on his own for the first time. He stayed for the first months with his uncle's family — his chacha — before eventually moving into a rented room in a house owned by a family who lived downstairs.

The room cost eighteen rupees a month.

His budget was thirty rupees a month — the proceeds from three months of vacation work as a stenographer at Edwards Mill, where the IAS officer Mangal Bihari Pande had needed a confidential assistant and the college principal had recommended the boy who had quietly listened from behind a door and then asked his mother's permission to take the job. His father had initially refused. *These mill owners — should we go and stand at attention for them? Should we do their service?* Kailash had argued, reasoned, persisted. He would go. He would take the job. Three months later, he had three hundred and thirty rupees, which was enough to fund a year.

The calculation in the rented room was elegant in its simplicity. Rent: eighteen rupees. That left twelve rupees for food, books, and everything else, for a year that included twelve months. It required discipline of a kind that most people in comfortable circumstances cannot fully imagine.

But then the family downstairs had children who needed tutoring, and Kailash had free afternoons, and so he began teaching them — not for payment, simply because they needed help and he was there. The children's grades improved. The family was grateful. And one afternoon the women of the house came upstairs, took away his flour container and his small collection of cooking supplies, and told him through the children: *"We will send your food up from now on."*

He did not have to ask for this. He did not expect it. It came, as so many things in his life would come, because he had given something first.

Rent: the family stopped charging him. Food: it arrived from below. On thirty rupees a month, he was managing his year in Ajmer with resources to spare.

## **Chapter Ten: Fifteen Kilometers a Day**

### *The Jaipur Walk*

For his B.Com Final, Kailash came to Jaipur, the state capital, and took up residence in the home of Jaswant Singh Ji Bhargav — a relative of the extended family, a Member of Parliament, father-in-law to a local political figure named Girdarlal Ji. The accommodation was comfortable enough, and Kailash maintained his practice of complete self-sufficiency: cooking his own meals, doing his own chores, managing his own affairs without burdening the household that hosted him.

The Commerce College was seven kilometers away.

He did not have a bicycle. He did not have money for bus fare. The bus cost four annas one way — a modest sum by any conventional accounting, but a sum that, multiplied across thirty days and two trips per day, added up to a meaningful fraction of the thirty rupees he had for the month. Every paisa counted. The math was simple and unambiguous: he would walk.

Seven kilometers in the morning. Seven kilometers in the afternoon. One more kilometer of movement around the college campus. Fifteen kilometers daily, in the heat of Jaipur summers and the chill of its winters, through the neighborhoods of Bhatta and past Harwa Mahal, down roads he would come to know better than his own face.

When people asked why he didn't take the bus, he had a ready answer: *"Bhai, exercise ho jati hai meri."* Brother, I get my exercise this way. It was true enough. It was also not the whole truth. The whole truth was that he was managing an entire year on resources that a middle-class family today might spend on a single dinner.

After two or three months, he made a trip home to Beawar and returned with a bicycle. The bicycle made the commute manageable in a different way — not eliminating the distance but transforming the relationship to it. He could coast on the downhills. He could feel the air move.

He was twenty years old, building a body and a character simultaneously, neither one aware of what it was becoming.

## **Chapter Eleven: The Brother's Debt**

### *Prem Bhai Sahab and the Power of Old Salt*

Kailash's elder brother, known as Prem Bhai Sahab, had come to Jaipur in 1956, six years before Kailash, under circumstances that beautifully illustrated the Bhargav family's recurring theme: the power of relationships built over decades and maintained across generations.

Prem Bhai Sahab had come for a job interview at Maharaja School — the school that sat across from the famous Hawa Mahal, the Palace of Winds. One of the interviewers was a man named Bhargavi, who happened to have studied in Jodhpur at the same time as Mahesh Prasad Bhargav, the boys' father, at Chopasani School. When Prem Bhai Sahab identified himself as Mahesh Prasad's son, the interviewer's face changed entirely.

*"Oh! He is my friend! You are his son?"*

*"Come tomorrow. The job is yours."*

No examination. No formal process. A father's friendship, maintained through the years, converting directly into a son's employment.

Prem Bhai Sahab worked at Maharaja School for five years, and during those years he also cultivated relationships with BD Bhargav, the Principal of Commerce College, and Sharda Bhargav, a Member of Parliament from the Rajya Sabha and a close associate of Jawaharlal Nehru. He gave them his skills for free — typing letters, preparing notes, the small secretarial work that made busy people's lives run more smoothly — and he expected nothing in return.

He was not calculating. He was simply generous. But generosity, in the Bhargav worldview, is not a one-time transaction. It is the construction of a relationship, and relationships have consequences that extend years and decades into the future.

When Prem Bhai Sahab left Jaipur for a government teaching post in Kishangarh, he and BD Bhargav parted as friends.

And then, some time later, Kailash Nath Bhargav noticed a job posting on the Commerce College notice board.

### *The Job That Came Without an Application*

The posting was for a Stenographer Instructor — someone qualified to teach shorthand, the system of rapid notation that allows trained writers to keep pace with normal speech. It was the kind of technical post that would usually require formal applications, references, and a selection process.

Kailash looked at the posting and went directly to BD Bhargav's office.

*"Sir,"* he said, *"my elder brother Prem Bhai Sahab — the one who used to do stenography work here — why don't you hire him?"*

BD Bhargav stared at him for a moment. Then: *"Arre bhai Kailash! I didn't even remember! I put out this advertisement for nothing!"*

The appointment letter was typed on the spot. Prem Bhai Sahab received a telephone call in Kishangarh that afternoon and arrived in Jaipur the next morning. He took one day's leave, collected his belongings from Kishangarh, and moved back to the city. The job was his before he'd had time to formally vacate the previous one.

For Kailash, this episode was not merely a family favor well-administered. It was confirmation of something he was coming to understand as a deep principle: that the way to secure your own future is not to pursue it directly, but to invest in others first. Help someone find a job. Give your skills freely. Eat someone's salt and remember it.

The world, if you have lived correctly, tends to remember too.

## **Chapter Twelve: The Road to Banking**

### *Shyamlal Ji and the Morning Walks*

In the building where Kailash lived after his brother joined Commerce College — the building belonging to Dr. Rishi's uncle, where they would spend four years — there lived an elder whom Kailash came to regard as a kind of professional mentor.

His name was Shyamlal Ji Bhargav. He had retired as General Manager of Jaipur Bank. He had eleven sons and one or two daughters. And every morning, he and Kailash would walk together through the streets of Jaipur in the early hours, the old man with his long career and the young man with his still-forming future, talking about the shape of things.

*"Kailash,"* Shyamlal Ji would say, *"join a bank. Any bank. A bank clerk's position is better than almost anything else you can do."*

He said this repeatedly. He said it from different angles, with different arguments — the salary, the security, the growth, the professional culture. Shyamlal Ji had spent his career in banking and believed in it the way that people believe in things they have lived fully. He was not making an abstract recommendation. He was telling this young man what he knew.

One morning, he became specific.

*"Union Bank has an interview tomorrow. You should go. I've already spoken to them. They will take you."*

### *Three Connections*

What followed was a sequence of relationships activating simultaneously — the kind of thing that looks, from the outside, like luck, but which on examination reveals itself as the accumulated fruit of a long season of goodwill.

Kailash went to his Mama Ji's house — his maternal uncle's. Mama Ji mentioned, as if in passing, that the assistant to the person who would be conducting Union Bank's appointments was his own companion in the Ramlila theatrical troupe — they performed together every year in the religious drama, playing parts together, building the particular closeness that comes from shared artistic effort.

*"Come,"* Mama Ji said. *"Let's go to his house this morning."*

So it was that two men in dhotis walked through the early morning streets of Jaipur and arrived at the home of Ambe Prasad Ji, who greeted them warmly, heard the situation, and said: *"Don't worry. The appointment will happen."*

At the interview itself, the process was, as Kailash describes it, a formality. A nominal test, nothing more. The decision had effectively been made by the network of relationships that surrounded him — Shyamlal Ji's recommendation, Mama Ji's friendship, Ambe Prasad Ji's assurance. He was offered the position and accepted it.

He had completed his B.Com the previous year. The very morning after his final examination — literally the next day — a different appointment had arrived: the NCC service position he had also been selected for. He had spent some time in that role before the banking opportunity presented itself. And now, as he stepped into Union Bank, he found himself exactly where the sum of his life had been pointing him.

He had not applied. He had not campaigned. He had built relationships, helped people, cultivated goodwill, and then — when the time came — been *selected*.

*"Main to dekho, pick karke liya hai service ke andar,"* he would say. *"Hamesh a maine koi chala ke apply nahi kiya. Automatic matlab saari cheezein aayi hai."*

Look — I was picked and brought into service. I never ran around applying. Everything came on its own.

## **Chapter Thirteen: The Philosophy**

### *What a Life Teaches*

Kailash Nath Bhargav has been many things across the decades. A boy who milked buffaloes at four in the morning. A young man who walked fifteen kilometers daily because he needed those four annas for something else. A basketball player who sweated through his shirts. An NCC sergeant calling parade commands at fourteen. An underofficer selected for military commission. A student who cooked his own meals in a single room and received hospitality in return for teaching children. A brother who found his sibling a job without being asked. A banker who received postings without requesting them.

He would tell you that these are not separate things. They are expressions of the same principle, applied across different domains.

The principle is this: *Jeevan mein doosron ka bhala chahta hai, uska bhala apne aap Bhagwan karta hai.* In life, whoever wishes well for others, God automatically does good for them.

This is not a passive principle. It does not mean sitting and waiting for divine intervention. It means walking the fifteen kilometers even when the bus would be faster, because your circumstances require the sacrifice and you will not complain about your circumstances. It means teaching the children downstairs even when no one asked you to, because they need help and you are there. It means picking up the telephone and telling the principal that your brother would be perfect for the job, even though no one asked for your opinion. It means eating someone's salt — whether you are a dacoit in the hills or a young man in a city — and remembering whose table you ate at.

The world is made of relationships. Relationships are made of acts, small and large, repeated over time. A reputation is a kind of accumulated capital, and Dewan Bahadur Pyarilal Ji's capital was still paying dividends forty years after his death when a bandit on a railway platform sent a family's kinsman safely home.

### *The Weight of Seven Generations*

Near the end of a long conversation with his grandchildren, conducted on a video call across continents, Kailash Nath Bhargav spoke about the importance of knowing where you come from.

*"Saat peedhi ki... dada dada ke dada dada ke dada... ye sab tumhari knowledge mein rahe."*

Seven generations. Your grandfather's grandfather's grandfather. All of this should remain in your knowledge.

He has a family chart. He wants to share it. He wants the names — Harbaksh Singh, Kanhaiya Lal, Sitaram Ji, the man who gave up his family to make a cousin's marriage possible, the man who built schools and gave away land and kept twenty-eight bighas for green — to be known by the children who carry that blood, even the ones who live far away and call in from computers.

This is not vanity. It is something else: a belief that the stories of the past are not ornaments but instructions. That knowing about Sitaram Ji's sacrifice teaches something about sacrifice. That knowing about Mor Singh's loyalty teaches something about loyalty. That knowing about the walks to school and the candle-making and the three hundred and thirty rupees teaches something about what a human being can do when the circumstances require it.

He is eighty-four years old as this is being written. He has lived across the full arc of modern Indian history — born under British rule, six years old at Independence, young adult during the wars with China and Pakistan, career built across the expansion of the Indian banking system, grandfather to children raised in a globalized world he could not have imagined from the rooftop of that haveli in Diggi Mohalla.

He is, by his own account, a man who never had to chase anything. But he is also, on the evidence of his life, a man who did everything that might make the chase unnecessary. He built his body in the fields at dawn. He built his character in the rented rooms of Ajmer and Jaipur. He built his network by teaching children, finding jobs for brothers, walking through early morning streets with retired bankers who had advice to give.

And at every turn, the world — with its contacts and its coincidences, its old school friends and its morning-walk companions — turned toward him and opened.

## **Epilogue: The Stories That Remain**

*"Das roz tak discuss karein to khatam nahi honge."*

If we discussed for ten days, the stories wouldn't finish.

There is still the marriage to be told — the baraat that traveled to Delhi, the wedding that assembled the branches of the Bhargav family from across the country. There is the banking career: the decades at Union Bank, the transfers from city to city, Jodhpur and Kota and Ajmer and Udaipur, the branch managership and the ceremonial opening with Mohan Singh Mehta, the former vice chancellor who remembered his student and gave him his public blessing. There are the colleagues and the crises, the promotions and the challenges, the slow building of a professional life that paralleled the building of a family.

And there is the question of what it all adds up to — what you keep when you have carried a life fully to its far edge and can look back across the whole of it.

Perhaps this: that the boy who could not climb the *chabutra* while his younger brother could — that boy grew up to understand that there are always things you cannot do, always moments of early defeat, and that what matters is not whether you can climb the platform on the first try, but whether you keep trying, and whether, while you are trying, you notice that someone else needs help getting up too.

Seven generations. Harbaksh Singh. Kanhaiya Lal. Sitaram Ji. Mahesh Prasad. Kailash Nath. And after him: the children and grandchildren scattered across cities and countries, carrying the name and the values, telling the stories, building the next chapter of a family history that will be, when it is told, the history of a people who believed that generosity is the most practical thing in the world.
